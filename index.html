Global Internet Access & Digital Economy

A visual story of global connectivity, digital inequality, and the economic opportunities created by internet access.

Global Internet Access and factors affecting it

This map shows global internet usage in 2024. Darker blue areas indicate countries with higher internet usage, while lighter green areas show lower levels of connectivity. The pattern highlights a clear digital divide between highly connected regions and countries where internet access remains limited.

The distribution shows that many countries have high internet usage in 2024, with a large concentration above 80%. However, some countries still remain in much lower usage ranges, showing that global connectivity is still uneven.

The chart highlights a clear digital divide across income groups in 2024. Countries with higher income levels tend to have much greater internet usage, while low-income countries show much lower connectivity, indicating that economic development is closely linked to access to digital infrastructure.

Internet usage has risen steadily across all income groups since 2000. The most notable change is the rapid growth of upper middle-income countries, which have almost caught up with high-income countries by 2025. However, the gap remains much wider for lower middle-income and low-income countries, showing that the global digital divide has narrowed at the top but still persists overall.

This bubble chart shows the relationship between GDP per capita and internet usage in 2024, with bubble size representing population. Countries with higher GDP per capita generally have higher internet usage, while countries with lower GDP per capita tend to have lower levels of connectivity. The chart suggests that economic development is strongly linked to digital access, although large-population countries can still show different patterns.

Countries with higher adult literacy rates generally show higher internet usage, suggesting a link between education and digital access. However, differences between countries indicate that other factors, such as infrastructure and income, also influence connectivity.

Digital Economy

ICT goods imports show a clear gap in global digital market scale. China and the United States stand far above most other countries, suggesting much larger technology demand supported by high internet usage and massive online markets. In contrast, many countries with lower imports and weaker internet penetration may still have smaller or less mature digital markets.

ICT service exports show that digital market strength is not only about hardware. Except for India, most top ICT service exporters also have high internet penetration, suggesting mature digital environments. India stands out despite its lower internet usage rate, reflecting its strong role in global ICT services and outsourcing.

As a share of GDP, ICT service exports show how dependent some economies are on digital services. Countries such as Israel and Ireland combine high export shares with high internet usage, suggesting mature digital service environments. India and some developing countries also stand out, showing that ICT services can become a major economic driver even when internet penetration is not equally high.

E-commerce market value shows how internet use turns into consumer market power. The United States and China dominate the global ranking, far ahead of other economies, suggesting that large connected populations and mature digital platforms create much stronger online markets. In comparison, European countries appear in the top 10 but at a much smaller scale.

This chart shows a clear positive relationship between estimated internet users and e-commerce market value. Economies with larger connected populations tend to have much bigger online markets, while high internet usage rates further support digital consumption. However, the gap between leading markets and smaller economies remains large, showing that internet access alone does not always translate equally into e-commerce scale.
